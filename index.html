Leadership, Bottlenecks, and Growing Great Teams

People in a team usually accept tasks when a leader assigns them. However, acceptance does not always translate into ownership or execution.

Are you the bottleneck in your team?

If you are the bottleneck, you may appear to be a strong leader—but in reality, this is a warning sign.

The Bottleneck Problem

When every decision depends on a single leader or architect, that person becomes a single point of failure. While this may feel like control, it limits team growth and scalability.

Some people avoid risk even when they know the solution. Others use bottleneck power for job security by withholding information, controlling access, and making themselves indispensable.

A Common Leadership Mistake

Early in leadership, many believe that protecting the team is the right approach—acting as a “superman” so the team can work peacefully.

  • Writing code for the team
  • Making all decisions alone
  • Shielding the team from challenges

Over time, this results in stagnant skills, comfort zones, and a lack of learning. This is the easiest way to be a leader—and the wrong one.

The True Role of a Leader

Your role as a leader is to make yourself unnecessary.

A successful leader removes themselves as a bottleneck by enabling others to solve problems independently.

If you are always busy, it often means you are solving the same problems repeatedly for different people.

By growing others, you will always have a job.

Coaching Instead of Solving

Instead of solving problems for the team, solve problems together. Share your thought process so others can learn how to approach similar challenges.

  • Grant decision-making authority
  • Coach people to make decisions
  • Challenge them to solve problems themselves

If leaders solve everything, the team never learns—and eventually stops trusting or depending on them.

Developing Individual Skills

Leaders must identify skill gaps, coach team members, and teach them how to face real-world challenges. Trust and responsibility are key drivers of growth.

Great Teams Are Grown, Not Hired

“Great teams are grown, not hired.”

Perfect teams do not exist. Teams are built from individuals with diverse backgrounds, experience levels, and perspectives. The leader’s role is to transform this group into a high-performing team.

Building a Team of Leaders

Create a team of leaders who work and grow together. When people are stuck, ask questions instead of providing answers immediately.

Even if the leader knows the solution, allowing the team to think and propose solutions leads to deeper learning.

Leadership Is Hard

Leadership done right is difficult—and that is why it matters. Taking responsibility, facing people directly, and continuously challenging the team are the hardest parts of leadership.

Learning and Growth Phases

Teams grow through three phases:

  • Survival
  • Learning
  • Self-organizing

Moving from survival to learning mode is challenging, but essential for long-term success.

Growing the team is our responsibility, our risk, our challenge, and our growth.
